# Writing Methods RAG Knowledge Base

Compact reference of story structure methods for QUANTUM Outliner retrieval.

## Save the Cat

Blake Snyder's 15-beat structure for screenwriting and novels.

Beats: Opening Image, Theme Stated, Set-Up, Catalyst, Debate, Break into Two, B Story, Fun and Games, Midpoint, Bad Guys Close In, All Is Lost, Dark Night of the Soul, Break into Three, Finale, Final Image.

Key principle: every scene must show the hero as someone the audience wants to root for ("save the cat" moment early). The Midpoint raises stakes with a false victory or false defeat. All Is Lost is the lowest emotional point. The Finale synthesizes both A and B stories.

Application: Use when plotting commercial fiction, romance, thriller, or any story that needs tight emotional pacing and clear act breaks.

## Hero's Journey

Joseph Campbell's monomyth as adapted by Christopher Vogler (The Writer's Journey).

Stages: Ordinary World, Call to Adventure, Refusal of the Call, Meeting the Mentor, Crossing the First Threshold, Tests/Allies/Enemies, Approach to the Inmost Cave, Ordeal, Reward, The Road Back, Resurrection, Return with the Elixir.

Key principle: transformation through departure, initiation, and return. The hero must face death (literal or symbolic) and be reborn changed. Each threshold crossing is irreversible.

Application: Epic fantasy, coming-of-age, adventure. Works best for single-protagonist external quests with internal transformation.

## Three-Act Structure

The foundational Western dramatic model. Act I (Setup, ~25%), Act II (Confrontation, ~50%), Act III (Resolution, ~25%).

Act I ends with the inciting incident locking the protagonist into the central conflict. Act II escalates through rising action, complications, and a midpoint reversal. Act III resolves through climax and denouement.

Key principle: each act ends with an irreversible turning point that raises stakes. The protagonist's goal must be clear by end of Act I.

Application: Universal foundation. Most other methods layer on top of three-act structure.

## Kishōtenketsu

Japanese/Chinese/Korean four-act structure without conflict as the driving force.

Parts: Ki (introduction) — establish characters and setting. Shō (development) — deepen without complication. Ten (twist) — introduce an unexpected element that recontextualizes. Ketsu (conclusion) — reconcile the twist with what came before.

Key principle: story emerges from juxtaposition and surprise rather than antagonist-driven conflict. The twist (ten) doesn't oppose the setup — it reveals hidden dimensions.

Application: Literary fiction, slice-of-life, manga/anime plotting, stories that explore theme over conflict, vignettes, philosophical narratives.

## Seven-Point Story Structure

Dan Wells' structure derived from the Star Wars model.

Points: Hook (opposite of Resolution), Plot Turn 1 (call to action), Pinch 1 (pressure/villain shows force), Midpoint (protagonist shifts from reaction to action), Pinch 2 (darkest moment, mentor dies or greatest loss), Plot Turn 2 (final piece of the puzzle), Resolution.

Key principle: work backwards from the Resolution to the Hook. Each pinch point forces the character to act. The midpoint is the fulcrum between reactive and proactive protagonist.

Application: Genre fiction, fantasy, sci-fi, thriller. Good for writers who know their ending first.

## Story Circle

Dan Harmon's simplified Hero's Journey in 8 steps, designed for episodic TV.

Steps: You (comfort zone), Need (desire), Go (unfamiliar situation), Search (adapt/struggle), Find (get what they wanted), Take (pay the price), Return (back to familiar), Change (they are different now).

Key principle: character movement between order and chaos. The bottom half of the circle (steps 5-8) mirrors the top. Every episode/chapter can be its own circle.

Application: Episodic fiction, TV writing, serial novels, web fiction, short stories that repeat with variation.

## Snowflake Method

Randy Ingermanson's fractal approach to novel planning.

Steps: One-sentence summary → one-paragraph summary → character summaries → expand paragraph to full page → character synopses → expand to four pages → character charts → scene list → scene descriptions.

Key principle: design before you write, expanding from abstract to concrete in controlled iterations. Each expansion step is 10x the previous detail level.

Application: Complex novels, multi-POV stories, writers who need to plan before drafting, series planning.

## Scene-Sequel

Dwight Swain's micro-structure for individual scene pairs.

Scene: Goal → Conflict → Disaster (things go wrong). Sequel: Reaction → Dilemma → Decision (leads to next scene's goal).

Key principle: scenes drive forward through action; sequels process emotion and make choices. Alternating creates rhythm. Disasters must logically follow from conflict. Decisions must be the least-bad option.

Application: Pacing control at the scene level. Commercial fiction, thriller, romance. Diagnose sagging middles by checking for missing sequels or disaster-free scenes.

## Fichtean Curve

Rising action from the very first page — no extended setup.

Structure: Open in media res with the first crisis. Each subsequent crisis escalates. Brief exposition woven between crises. Climax is the highest crisis. Falling action is minimal.

Key principle: the reader never gets a comfortable resting place. Each crisis forces a harder choice. Exposition is earned by making the reader curious through action first.

Application: Thriller, literary fiction with urgency, short stories, flash fiction, opening chapters of longer works.

## The Story Spine

Kenn Adams' improv-based eight-sentence structure.

Template: Once upon a time... / Every day... / But one day... / Because of that... / Because of that... / Because of that... / Until finally... / And ever since then...

Key principle: causality chain. Every beat follows from the previous through "because of that" logic. The "but one day" is the inciting incident. "Until finally" is the climax.

Application: Quick story testing, oral storytelling, picture books, pitch development, checking if a plot has genuine causality.

## MICE Quotient

Orson Scott Card's framework for classifying story threads by their core question.

Types: Milieu (character enters unfamiliar place → escapes/masters it), Idea (question raised → answered), Character (dissatisfaction → new role or acceptance), Event (disruption → new order or restored order).

Key principle: stories nest MICE threads like HTML tags — first opened, last closed. Opening a thread creates a promise; the story feels unresolved until that thread closes.

Application: Diagnosing structural problems, managing subplots, understanding why a story feels unfinished, balancing multiple narrative threads.

## Try-Fail Cycles

The principle that protagonists must attempt solutions that fail before succeeding.

Pattern: Attempt 1 (obvious solution, fails) → Attempt 2 (clever solution, partial success but new problem) → Attempt 3 (unexpected solution using everything learned, succeeds or fails definitively).

Key principle: each failure must teach something and raise stakes. "Yes, but..." and "No, and..." responses create escalation. Straight success is unsatisfying; straight failure is depressing.

Application: Action sequences, mystery solving, romance obstacles, any scene where the protagonist pursues a goal. Minimum two failures before climactic resolution.

## Promises and Payoffs

Brandon Sanderson's contract model between author and reader.

Principle: early story elements (tone, magic system rules, character traits, imagery) create implicit promises. The ending must pay off those promises or subvert them in a satisfying way. Chekhov's Gun is one instance of this principle.

Categories: Tone promises (comedy stays comic), Character promises (flaws will be tested), World promises (established rules will matter in the climax), Genre promises (romance ends in union or clear separation).

Application: Revision tool. List every promise made in Act I, verify each is paid off by the end. Identify accidental promises (details that seem significant but go nowhere).

## Negative Space Storytelling

Telling through omission, implication, and what characters don't say.

Techniques: Unreliable narration, time skips over key events, subtext-heavy dialogue, environmental storytelling, conspicuous absence, iceberg theory (Hemingway).

Key principle: what's left unsaid carries more emotional weight than exposition. The reader's imagination fills gaps more powerfully than the writer can. Trust the reader.

Application: Literary fiction, horror, mystery, minimalist prose, short fiction, any scene where direct statement would reduce impact.
